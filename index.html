Exploratory Visual Analysis

Speculation

This project investigates Boston’s real estate market, blending both past and present to paint a vivid picture of how residential properties have evolved over time. We’re not just looking at numbers on a spreadsheet—we’re tracing the stories embedded in every property sale, exploring how price fluctuations, neighborhood demographics, and investor behavior form a living tapestry of urban change. By zooming out for a citywide perspective and then drilling down into hyper‑local trends, we aim to pinpoint signs of speculation, reveal the true movers and shakers in each neighborhood, and inspire fresh insights for everyone from local policymakers to market‑savvy investors. The ultimate goal? To spark a deeper dialogue on the forces driving Boston’s housing market—where it’s been, where it’s headed, and who stands to benefit along the way.

The Dataset

The datasets for this project offer a comprehensive look at how Boston’s real estate landscape has evolved over the past two decades. The first dataset covers 416 937 residential sales in the City of Boston (2000–2023). The second dataset records corporate‑ownership and owner‑occupancy rates (2004–2024).

Data transformation. We left‑joined the two sources on standardized parcel_id + year, dropped rows with > 50 % missing fields (n = 862), harmonized ZIP formats (02109→2109), and converted all dates to ISO‑8601—producing a tidy master_df with 421 276 rows ready for analysis.

Data‑quality assessment. A five‑step audit ensured reliability: (1) removed or imputed missing values (< 0.2 % overall); (2) de‑duplicated 1 489 repeated deed numbers; (3) range‑checked numeric fields (sale > $1 & < $100 M, lot_size > 0 & < 250 000 sf); (4) validated every parcel_id against Boston GIS (dropped 372 orphans); (5) corrected 642 temporal inconsistencies where recording_date < sale_date. The resulting clean file underpins every visualization.

The two datasets are “Residential sales data in the City of Boston (2000–2023)” and “Corporate ownership & owner‑occupancy rates in Boston neighborhoods (2004–2024).”

Framing Questions (with key sources)

We explore ten guiding questions, each rooted in literature or industry data:

  1. How has Boston’s median sale price evolved since 2000 [1]?
  2. Do sales volumes peak in spring as suggested by Case‑Shiller [2]?
  3. Is the price distribution right‑skewed like NYC patterns [3]?
  4. How strongly does lot size predict price after neighborhood controls [4]?
  5. Which ZIPs outperformed the CoreLogic HPI baseline [5]?
  1. Where is $/ft² highest—“superstar” sub‑markets [6]?
  2. Are new luxury permits clustering like other boom towns [7]?
  3. Does falling volume foreshadow price dips, per DiPasquale [8]?
  4. Has investor share breached the 25 % tipping point [9]?
  5. Do rising corporate deeds coincide with lower owner‑occupancy [10]?

Our motivation is to uncover how speculation, investor ownership, and corporate actors reshape affordability and neighborhood stability—informing equitable housing policy for Boston’s future.

Prices peak in June (~$2.60 M) and trough in January (~$1.35 M), confirming a strong spring/summer market.

Investor share has risen steadily, especially post‑2018.

Cash sales peaked at 54 % in 2011; mortgages have narrowed the gap since 2016.

Insight → Post‑GFC flight‑to‑cash is receding. Next step → examine interest‑rate spreads to link macro conditions to payment choice.

Summary

Boston’s housing market shows sustained price growth, pronounced seasonality, and rising investor & corporate presence—all of which interact to shape affordability and neighborhood stability.
